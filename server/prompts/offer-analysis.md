# $100M Offers: Offer Messaging Playbook

Based on Alex Hormozi's $100M Offers methodology — focused on how to **communicate** an offer so persuasively that people feel stupid saying no.

---

## Core Philosophy

"Make people an offer so good they would feel stupid saying no."

The offer itself is only half the battle. How you communicate it — the words you choose, the order you reveal it, the emotions you trigger — determines whether the prospect actually *feels* that "stupid saying no" sensation. A great offer poorly communicated is a missed opportunity. This framework produces **the exact language and scripted sequences** that make prospects feel the offer in their gut, not just understand it in their head.

**The Value Equation — the lens behind every word you write:**

```
                Dream Outcome  x  Perceived Likelihood
Value =    ──────────────────────────────────────────────
                Time Delay  x  Effort & Sacrifice

MAXIMIZE the numerator.    MINIMIZE the denominator.
```

Every sentence in your messaging should move at least one of these four levers. If a sentence doesn't make the dream bigger, the belief stronger, the timeline shorter, or the effort smaller — cut it.

---

## How to Use This Framework

This is a **messaging framework**, not an internal analysis document. You'll analyze the business, but every phase outputs **language the prospect hears** — scripts, phrases, framings, and sequences. The test for every output: *could someone read this aloud to a prospect and have it land?*

**Do NOT skip phases or jump to scripting prematurely.** Phases 1-2 build the emotional foundation. Phase 3 onward builds the communication. Without understanding their pain in their own words first, everything else rings hollow.

### Input Required

Before beginning, gather:

- **What they sell** (product/service, current offer structure)
- **Who they sell to** (target customer/avatar)
- **Current pricing** (what they charge now)
- **How they deliver** (fulfillment model)
- **Current pain points** (what's not working — low conversion, thin margins, commoditization)
- **Market context** (competitors, what alternatives prospects have tried)

## Phase 1: Prospect Identity & Voice

"The point of good persuasion is for the prospect to feel understood."

Before you can speak to someone persuasively, you need their vocabulary — not yours, *theirs*. This phase builds the voice profile that every subsequent phase draws from.

### Map Their Identity

| Question | What to Capture |
|----------|----------------|
| **Who are they?** | Role, title, self-image — how they introduce themselves |
| **What identity do they hold?** | "I'm a hustler" / "I'm a provider" / "I'm the expert" — what matters to their self-concept |
| **What identity do they want?** | Who they're trying to become — the aspirational self |
| **What words do they use for their problem?** | Not your clinical description — their frustrated, late-night language |
| **What have they tried that failed?** | Past solutions, past disappointments, past money wasted |
| **What are they sick of hearing?** | The cliches and empty promises from your competitors |
| **Where do they congregate?** | Communities, forums, events — and what language is used there |

### Output: Voice Profile

Write 10-15 phrases the prospect actually says about their situation. These are the raw materials for every script that follows.

**Example — agency owner selling to local businesses:**

- NOT: "Small business owners experiencing challenges with digital customer acquisition"
- YES:
  - "I'm spending $3K/month on ads and I have no idea if it's working."
  - "Every agency says the same thing — 'give it 90 days.' I've given three of them 90 days."
  - "I just want someone who actually gives a shit about my business."
  - "My nephew said he could do my social media. It went about how you'd expect."
  - "I know I need marketing. I just don't know who to trust anymore."

These phrases become the mirror you hold up in Phases 2-4. When the prospect hears their own words reflected back, they feel understood — and understanding is the foundation of persuasion.

### Niche Specificity Check

The more specific your prospect definition, the more specific (and persuasive) your language. Apply the niching test:

| Niche Level | Language Precision | Price Power |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Generic: "Business owners" | Vague, forgettable | Low |
| Industry: "Dentists" | Starting to resonate | Medium |
| Sub-niche: "Dentists scaling to a second location" | They feel seen | High |
| Hyper-specific: "Dentists in suburbs scaling to location #2 who've been burned by agencies" | They feel *understood* | Premium |

"You can literally charge a hundred times more for the exact same product" — because the messaging precision makes it feel custom-built for them.

## Phase 2: Pain Amplification & Contrast Framing

Purpose: Make the prospect feel the full weight of their current situation *before* you offer anything. If they don't feel the pain, they won't value the cure.

### Step 1: Map the Customer Journey

List every step the customer takes before, during, and after engaging with the product/service. Think about what happens immediately before and after each interaction.

### Step 2: Surface Problems at Each Step

For each journey step, generate problems through all four value lenses:

| Lens | Prompt |
|------|--------|
| **Dream Outcome** | "This won't be worth it because..." / "I won't get the result because..." |
| **Perceived Likelihood** | "This won't work for *me* specifically because..." / "My situation is different because..." |
| **Effort & Sacrifice** | "This will be too hard/confusing/painful because..." / "I'll have to give up X..." |
| **Time Delay** | "This will take too long because..." / "I need results by..." |

**Target: 20-50+ distinct problems.** Think like the most skeptical, overwhelmed, twice-burned version of the prospect.

### Step 3: Write Pain Amplification Language

For each major problem, write the sentence that makes them feel understood. Three layers:

1. **Surface pain** — what they'd say if you asked "what's the problem?"
2. **Deeper pain** — what it actually costs them (time, money, stress, opportunity, relationships)
3. **Identity pain** — what it says about them that they haven't solved this yet

**Pain language formula:**
> "You know that feeling when [specific painful scenario they recognize]? And then [consequence they don't say out loud]? That's not a [excuse they tell themselves] problem — that's a [real problem] problem."

**Example — weight loss:**
- Surface: "I can't lose the weight."
- Deeper: "I've spent $4,000 on programs and I'm heavier than when I started."
- Identity: "I'm starting to think I'm just not someone who can do this."
- Pain language: "You've started over so many times you've lost count. And the worst part isn't the money — it's that quiet voice that says 'maybe this is just who I am.' That voice is wrong. But I understand why it's loud right now."

### Step 4: Build the Contrast Frame

For each major pain point, write the before/after in vivid, daily-life terms:

| Their World Now | Their World After |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| *[Specific, visceral daily reality]* | *[Specific, visceral daily reality]* |

**Example — agency owner selling lead gen:**

| Their World Now | Their World After |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Checking your phone at dinner hoping for leads that don't come | Your calendar has tomorrow's calls already booked before you leave the office |
| Writing proposals for people who ghost you | Prospects showing up pre-sold, asking "how do we get started?" |
| Telling your spouse "next month will be different" again | Planning the vacation you've been postponing for two years |

The gap between these two columns is the emotional engine of your entire offer.

### Step 5: Calculate the Cost of Inaction

Give the pain a price tag:

> "Every month you don't solve this, you're [specific loss]. That's not theoretical — over the next year, that's [annual number]. And that's just the money. That doesn't count [emotional/relationship/opportunity cost]."

## Phase 3: Dream Outcome Messaging

Purpose: Paint the result so vividly they can taste it. Don't sell the product — sell the destination.

"No one wants a gym membership. They want to look good naked."

### The Destination Statement

Write 2-3 versions of the core promise. Rules:
- **Specific, not vague** — "double your close rate from 15% to 30%" not "grow your business"
- **Status-laden** — how will others see them differently? "Talk in terms of things your prospect believes will increase their status."
- **Sensory** — what will their day actually look and feel like?

### Future Pacing Script

Write a 3-5 sentence visualization that puts them *inside* the result:

> "Imagine [timeframe] from now. You [specific morning routine change]. You check [specific metric] and see [specific number]. Your [spouse/team/peers] notice because [specific behavioral change]. That's not hypothetical — that's what [proof reference] experienced by [timeframe]."

**Example:**
> "Picture this: 90 days from now, you wake up and check your dashboard. There are 14 qualified appointments booked for the week — and you didn't chase a single one. Your phone isn't blowing up because the system is running. Your business partner asks what changed. That's not a fantasy — that's what happened for [client name] in 11 weeks."

### Identity Shift

Articulate who they *become*, not just what they get:

> "You stop being the [old identity — the one who struggles, chases, worries] and become the [new identity — the one who has the system, the certainty, the results]."

People don't just buy outcomes — they buy the version of themselves that has those outcomes.

## Phase 4: Proof & Story Architecture

Purpose: Make them believe *they specifically* will get this result. Perception is reality — "it's not about actual likelihood, but the prospect's belief."

### Story Framework

Every proof point is a micro-story, not a stat. Stats inform. Stories convince.

**Structure:**
> "[Name/role similar to prospect] came to us [situation that mirrors prospect's exact pain]. They had already [failed attempts prospect will recognize]. Within [timeframe], they [specific measurable result]. The thing that surprised them most was [unexpected benefit that addresses a secondary objection]."

**Example:**
> "Sarah ran a dental practice in a suburb outside Atlanta — two hygienists, one associate, drowning in overhead. She'd tried two agencies and an in-house marketing hire. Nothing moved the needle. Within 60 days of starting with us, she had 47 new patient bookings. But what she didn't expect was that her *existing* patients started referring more — because the positioning work changed how people talked about her practice."

### Proof Stack

Layer three types of proof, each targeting a different level of skepticism:

1. **Volume proof** — "We've done this for X people/companies" — establishes you're not experimenting
2. **Similarity proof** — "Here's someone exactly like you" — neutralizes "my situation is different"
3. **Mechanism proof** — "Here's specifically why this works" — the named system or process that makes results feel predictable, not lucky

### Credibility Statement

One sentence that earns the right to be heard. Not a bio dump — a single proof of authority:

> "I [specific relevant credential or result that's hard to argue with] — which is the only reason I can promise [the specific thing you're promising]."

## Phase 5: Price Framing

Purpose: Make the price feel inevitable and small — not by lowering it, but by building the frame around it.

"There is no strategic benefit to being the second cheapest in the marketplace, but there is for being the most expensive." — Dan Kennedy

### The Anchoring Sequence

Before you ever say the price, establish these anchors *in this order*:

1. **Cost of inaction** (from Phase 2): "You told me this problem is costing you [$X/month]. That's [$Y/year]."
2. **Failed solution spend**: "You've already invested [$Z] on [things that didn't work]. That money is gone."
3. **Alternative cost**: "You could hire a [full-time employee / consultant / competitor] for [$high number/month], and you'd still have to [gap they wouldn't fill]."
4. **Total stack value**: "The total value of everything I'm about to walk you through is [$stack total]."

Each anchor makes the eventual price feel smaller by comparison.

### The Price Reveal Script

> "The total value of everything I just described is [stack total]. If I charged [fraction of that], it would still pay for itself in [timeframe]. But I'm not charging anywhere close to that. Your investment is [price]."

Pause. Let silence do the work.

### ROI Framing

Give them the math they'll use to justify it to themselves — or their spouse, their partner, their board:

> "That's [price per day/week/month]. You make that back the moment [specific, believable scenario that recoups the cost]. Everything after that is profit."

### Pricing Psychology (Internal)

- **The Virtuous Cycle:** Higher price → higher emotional investment → better results → more proof → justifies higher price
- **"Those who pay the most, pay the most attention."** Low price attracts low-commitment clients who get worse results.
- **Demand is fractal:** "One-fifth of prospects are willing to pay five times the price or more." There is always a premium tier.
- **Wine Study:** Researchers gave identical wine at three price points. Tasters consistently rated the "expensive" wine highest. Price *changes* perception of quality.

## Phase 6: Stack Presentation Script

Purpose: Present the offer components as a scripted performance, not a list. "A single offer is less valuable than the same offer broken into its component parts and stacked."

### Convert Solutions to Named Components

Take the solutions from Phase 2's problem mapping. For each one you're keeping in the offer, apply the naming formula:

**MAGIC Naming:**
| Letter | Component | Purpose |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **M** | Magnetic Reason Why | Why it exists |
| **A** | Announce Your Avatar | Who it's for |
| **G** | Give Them a Goal | Dream outcome |
| **I** | Indicate Time Interval | Duration |
| **C** | Complete with Container Word | Bundle type: Blueprint, System, Accelerator, Toolkit, Sprint |

Use rhyme ("Six-Pack Fast Track") or alliteration ("Profit Playbook") when possible.

### Script Each Stack Item

For each component, write four beats:

1. **Setup** — Name the problem it solves using pain language from Phase 2
   > "Now, one of the biggest things I hear from [avatar] is [problem in their words]..."
2. **Reveal** — Introduce the component by its benefit-driven name
   > "That's why we built the [Named Component]."
3. **Anchor** — What this would cost on its own
   > "If you hired someone to build this for you, you'd pay [$X]. We're including it."
4. **Bridge** — Transition to the next item
   > "But even with that, there's still the question of [next problem]..."

### Running Total Momentum

Build the "wall of value" with escalating energy:

> "So that's the [Component 1] — a [$X] value. But I also want to make sure you [solve pain that Component 2 addresses], which is why you're also getting [Component 2]. That alone is worth [$Y]. So we're already at [$X+Y] in value, and I haven't even gotten to the part that [teases most exciting component]..."

### The Stack Summary

After all components, recap the full stack:

| # | Component | Value |
|---|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Core: [Name] | $X,XXX |
| 2 | [Name] | $X,XXX |
| 3 | [Name] | $X,XXX |
| ... | ... | ... |
| | **Total Value** | **$XX,XXX** |
| | **Your Investment Today** | **$X,XXX** |

The total perceived value should be **5-10x the asking price**.

## Phase 7: Bonus Reveal Sequence

Purpose: Drop bonuses as objection-neutralizers timed to the moments doubt surfaces.

### Design 3-7 Bonuses

For each bonus, complete this:

| Element | What to Write |
|---------|---------------|
| **Objection it neutralizes** | The specific doubt in the prospect's mind at this moment |
| **Transition language** | "Now, some people at this point think [objection in their words] — which is exactly why I created..." |
| **Name** | Benefit-driven title (MAGIC naming) |
| **What it is** | One-sentence deliverable |
| **Why it matters** | Which value driver it moves (reduces effort? shortens timeline? increases belief?) |
| **Value anchor** | "If you bought this separately / hired someone for this, you'd pay [$X]" |
| **Proof snippet** | One sentence: a result or reason this works |

### Bonus Principles

- **Tools over training** — checklists, templates, swipe files beat more courses. Lower perceived effort = higher perceived value.
- **Each bonus should address a specific obstacle** from Phase 2's problem list
- **Total bonus value should exceed core offer value** — makes the deal feel absurd
- **"Solve the next problem"** — anticipate what they'll need after the core offer delivers, and include it

### Reveal Order Strategy

**In a 1-on-1 conversation:**
1. Ask for the sale FIRST
2. If YES → reveal bonuses as a delightful surprise: "Oh — and I almost forgot, you're also getting..."
3. If hesitation → present a specific bonus matching their stated concern, then ask again
4. Repeat with different bonuses for different objections

**In a presentation / webinar:**
1. Present bonuses after the stack, before the price
2. Each bonus should feel like another layer of "this is ridiculous"
3. Bridge between bonuses: "And because I know [next objection], I'm also including..."

### Partner Bonuses (High Leverage)

Get complementary businesses to provide their products/services as your bonuses:
- Free marketing for them (exposure to your audience)
- High perceived value for your clients at zero cost to you
- Negotiate group discounts or referral commissions

## Phase 8: Scarcity & Urgency Scripting

Purpose: Create honest reasons to act now, and script exactly how to communicate them.

"Fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain."

### Scarcity (Quantity-Based)

Pick the most honest mechanism:

| Type | When It's Real |
|------|---------------|
| **Capacity cap** | You literally can't serve more at this quality level |
| **Cohort cap** | Group size affects the experience |
| **Bonus scarcity** | Limited supply of bonus resources (1-on-1 calls, physical items) |

**Script it:**
> "[I / we] can only take on [X] clients at this level — that's not a marketing trick, it's a [staffing / attention / quality] constraint. Right now there are [specific number] spots. When they fill, the next opening is [specific timeframe]. I'd rather turn people away than water down what we deliver."

**Rules:**
- Must be real — manufactured scarcity destroys trust
- Always communicate when sold out (creates social proof for next round)
- "Selling out consistently" is more powerful than "always available"

### Urgency (Time-Based)

Pick the most honest mechanism:

| Type | When It's Real |
|------|---------------|
| **Cohort start date** | Groups start together on a fixed date |
| **Price increase** | You're genuinely raising prices |
| **Seasonal enrollment** | You run this at specific times per year |
| **Exploding opportunity** | Market conditions, regulatory changes, arbitrage windows |

**Script it:**
> "This [pricing / bonus package / enrollment window] closes on [specific date and time]. Not because I'm trying to pressure you — but because [honest reason]. After [deadline], [what specifically changes: price goes up / bonuses disappear / you wait until next quarter]. If you're in, now is the time."

**The Last-Hour Effect:** 50-60% of sales in a campaign happen in the last 4 hours. Real deadlines compress action. Don't apologize for having them.

## Phase 9: Risk Reversal Language

Purpose: Make the guarantee feel like the prospect has nothing to lose and everything to gain.

"The single greatest objection is risk."

### Choose Your Guarantee Type

| Type | Core Message | Best For |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Unconditional** | "Try it. Don't love it? Full refund. No questions, no hoops." | Cold audiences, building initial trust |
| **Conditional** | "Do [specific actions] for [timeframe]. If you don't get [specific result], I [refund / refund double / keep working free]." | Filtering for serious clients, high-ticket |
| **Anti-Guarantee** | "All sales are final — and here's why you should be *excited* about that: [compelling reason]." | Extreme demand, consumable access |
| **Performance** | "You don't pay until [milestone]. If I don't deliver, you owe nothing." | Service businesses, agencies |

### Stacking Guarantees (Power Move)

Combine types for maximum risk reversal:

> **The [Named] Guarantee:**
> Try it for 30 days. If you don't love it for any reason, full refund — no questions asked.
> PLUS: Complete the full program over 90 days. If you do the work and don't hit [specific result], I refund triple your investment.

### Name the Guarantee

A named guarantee is more memorable than "money-back guarantee":
- "The Sleep Like a Baby Guarantee"
- "The Triple-Your-Money Freedom Guarantee"
- "The No-Weasel-Clauses Guarantee"

### Risk Reversal Script

> "Here's how confident I am: [guarantee terms in plain language]. The way I see it, you have two options. Option one: do nothing, and [cost of inaction from Phase 2] keeps compounding. Option two: try this, and if it works, [dream outcome]. If it doesn't, [what they get back]. There's literally no scenario where you come out behind. The only real risk is doing nothing."

### The Math (For Your Confidence)

If a guarantee helps close 30% more people, and refunds double from 5% to 10%:
- Without: 100 sales x $1,000 = $100K - $5K refunds = **$95K**
- With: 130 sales x $1,000 = $130K - $13K refunds = **$117K**
- **Net: $22K more profit.** The guarantee isn't generosity — it's a conversion tool.

## Phase 10: Objection Reframing Arsenal

Purpose: Prepare conversational pivots for every objection *before* you hear them. The best salespeople aren't fast on their feet — they've already thought through every response.

### Build the Arsenal

For each objection the prospect might raise:

| Element | What to Write |
|---------|---------------|
| **The objection (their exact words)** | What they'd actually say — not your sanitized version |
| **What they actually mean** | The real fear underneath the stated objection |
| **The reframe** | 1-2 sentences that pivot their perspective |
| **The proof** | Story or data point that supports the reframe |

### Reframe Patterns

**"That's exactly why"** — Turns the objection into a reason to buy:
> "You're too busy? That's exactly why you need this — it's built for people who don't have time to figure this out on their own."

**"What if the opposite is true?"** — Challenges the underlying assumption:
> "What if the reason it hasn't worked before is *precisely because* you've been trying to do it the cheap way?"

**"The real cost"** — Redirects from price to cost of inaction:
> "I hear you on the investment. But you told me this problem is costing you [$X/month]. In six months, that's [$6X]. This pays for itself in [timeframe]."

**"Acknowledge + pivot"** — Validates their concern, then reframes:
> "I totally get that — honestly, the people who get the best results from this are the ones who felt exactly that way before they started."

**"The reason you feel that way"** — Names the real barrier:
> "The reason you're hesitant isn't really [stated objection]. It's that you've been burned before, and you don't want to waste money again. I get it. That's why [guarantee / proof / mechanism]."

### Common Objections to Prepare For

- "I need to think about it"
- "I need to talk to [spouse / partner / board]"
- "I can't afford it right now"
- "I've tried something like this before"
- "What makes you different from [competitor]?"
- "Can I start next month / quarter?"
- "What if it doesn't work for my [specific situation]?"

Write a reframe for each that's relevant to this specific business.

## Phase 11: Offer Naming (MAGIC Formula)

Create 3-5 name options using M.A.G.I.C.:

| Letter | Component | Purpose |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **M** | Magnetic Reason Why | Why the promotion exists: Free, Grand Opening, Anniversary, New Year |
| **A** | Announce Your Avatar | Who it's for: "Towson Maryland Moms", "B2B SaaS Founders" |
| **G** | Give Them a Goal | Dream outcome: Pain-Free, Double Your Revenue, Celebrity Smile |
| **I** | Indicate Time Interval | Duration: 6-Week, 21-Day, 90-Day, 4-Hour |
| **C** | Complete with Container Word | Bundle type: Challenge, Blueprint, Boot Camp, Accelerator, System, Sprint |

### Rules
- Use 3-5 components (not always all 5)
- Shorter and punchier beats longer
- Use rhyme when possible ("Six-Pack Fast Track", "Marriage Thrive Deep Dive")
- Use alliteration when possible ("Make Money Masterclass", "Profit Playbook")
- Apply MAGIC to bonuses and sub-components too

### When Offers Fatigue (Refresh In This Order)
1. **Creative** — new images/visuals (easiest, cheapest)
2. **Body copy** — rewrite the ad text
3. **Headline/MAGIC name** — new wrapper, same offer
4. **Duration** — change time interval
5. **Enhancer** — change the free/discount component
6. **Monetization model** — restructure how they pay (last resort)

## Key Principles

- **"The pain is the pitch."** If they feel the pain deeply enough, the solution sells itself.
- **"Fast beats free."** People pay premiums for speed. Communicate time-to-result early and often.
- **"Done-for-you > Done-with-you > Do-it-yourself"** — in perceived value. Position accordingly.
- **"Those who pay the most, pay the most attention."** High price is a feature, not a bug.
- **"The longer you delay the ask, the bigger the ask you can make."** Build value before revealing price.
- **"Your offer sucks. Not you."** If it doesn't convert, fix the messaging and the offer structure — not yourself.
- **"We only want things we do not have."** If you seek to increase demand, decrease or delay satisfying the desire.
- **"Create flow. Monetize flow. Then add friction."** Start irresistible, then optimize.
