# Warm Email Sequence Generator — Instructions for AI

Instructions for creating a warm email launch sequence for any Kodara client, given their offer reference material.

**Reference implementations:** `maverick-warm-emails.md` (content) and `maverick-warm-emails.html` (styled HTML) in this folder.
**Design system:** `DESIGN_SYSTEM.md` in this folder (for the HTML version).

---

## 1. Overview

This is a **10-email warm launch sequence** that:

- Targets the client's existing network (people who already know them)
- Builds belief before introducing the product
- Uses a "reply to this email" CTA on every email (no links, no landing pages)
- Follows a proven 25-day cadence from awareness → belief → offer → urgency → close
- Is personal, conversational, and peer-to-peer in tone
- Can be output as markdown (`.md`) for copy use AND as styled HTML (`.html`) using the Kodara design system

---

## 2. Source Material

The email sequence draws from the offer file. Key sections to extract:

```
FROM THE OFFER FILE:
- Context sections 1-8 (client, audience, problems, credibility, market)
- Product concept (what it does, who it's for, differentiators)
- Belief Ladder (pain, pointing, selling)
- Pricing strategy (price, anchors, payment plans, launch pricing)
- Package details (inclusions, exclusions, complete product)
- Warm Lead Messaging section (if present — use as direct reference for email subjects and arguments)
```

If the offer file includes a "PART 2: WARM LEAD MESSAGING" section with a 7-email sequence, **expand it to 10 emails** by deepening the belief-building phase (emails 1-3) and adding a "what 30 days looks like" future-pacing email and a final respectful close.

## 3. Email-by-Email Structure

### Email Sequence Arc

| Phase | Emails | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| **Belief Building** | 1-3 | Build awareness of the problem and opportunity without mentioning the product |
| **Product Introduction** | 4-6 | Introduce the product, explain what's included, justify the price |
| **Objection Handling + Close** | 7-10 | Handle objections, future-pace, and drive action with urgency |

### Email 1: The Big Opportunity

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 1 |
| **Focus** | Desire — opportunity awareness |
| **Subject line** | Curiosity-driven, references the biggest stat or market shift |
| **Content** | Lead with the most compelling market stat. Show the gap between what's available and what the reader is accessing. End with a teaser — "we're working on something" |
| **CTA** | "If [problem resonates], reply and let me know. I want to make sure you're one of the first to hear what we've built." |

### Email 2: The Real Problem

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 3 |
| **Focus** | Pain — validation of the struggle |
| **Subject line** | Reframes blame away from the reader (it's not their fault) |
| **Content** | Name the actual bottleneck (usually capacity, not capability). Use specific numbers — hours per task, volume required, staff limitations. Show the cascade of consequences. |
| **CTA** | "If this hits close to home — reply and tell me [specific question about their situation]." |

### Email 3: What the Winners Are Doing

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 5 |
| **Focus** | Pointing — the general approach (not the product yet) |
| **Subject line** | Implies others are getting ahead |
| **Content** | Reference early adopters getting results with the general approach (AI, systems, automation — whatever applies). Use specific result numbers. Create FOMO without being manipulative. |
| **CTA** | "If you want to see how this actually works, reply and I'll send you a walkthrough." |

### Email 4: Why We Built This (Origin Story)

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 8 |
| **Focus** | Product introduction — origin story |
| **Subject line** | Reveals a limitation or honest admission |
| **Content** | Tell the story of WHY this product exists. Usually: "we couldn't serve enough people the old way, so we built something." Introduce the product by name. Differentiate from generic alternatives. |
| **CTA** | "Reply with '[KEYWORD]' and I'll send you a real example." |

### Email 5: What You Actually Get

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 10 |
| **Focus** | Product details — full breakdown |
| **Subject line** | "Here's everything inside (no surprises)" or similar transparency signal |
| **Content** | List EVERY inclusion from the offer with descriptions. Group by category. Include pricing at the bottom (founding/launch rate + regular rate). |
| **CTA** | "Reply with any questions — or if you want me to walk you through it personally." |

### Email 6: The Price Comparison

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 13 |
| **Focus** | Value anchor — price comparison math |
| **Subject line** | Juxtaposes the product price against an expensive alternative |
| **Content** | Present 3-4 alternatives with their costs (Option A, B, C, D format). Show the math. End with "pays for itself with [smallest win]." Frame as augmentation, not replacement. |
| **CTA** | "Reply if you want to talk through what this would look like for your specific situation." |

### Email 7: The Cost of Inaction

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 16 |
| **Focus** | Pain + urgency — what inaction costs |
| **Subject line** | References a missed opportunity or deadline |
| **Content** | Ask about missed opportunities. Paint a specific scenario of a missed [deadline/client/sale]. Multiply across a year. Give a conservative dollar estimate of what inaction costs. Mention founding rate is filling up. |
| **CTA** | "Reply and tell me — what's the one [thing] you missed that still bothers you?" |

### Email 8: Objection Handling

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 19 |
| **Focus** | Specificity — "will this work for me?" |
| **Subject line** | States the objection directly ("Does it actually work for [my situation]?") |
| **Content** | Acknowledge the #1 objection honestly. Explain why generic alternatives fail. List specific knowledge/capabilities the product has. Be honest about limitations ("Is it perfect every time? No.") but show how support fills the gap. |
| **CTA** | "Reply and tell me [your specific need]. I'll show you exactly what the tool would produce. No charge, no commitment." |

### Email 9: Future Pacing (30-Day Walkthrough)

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 22 |
| **Focus** | Onboarding vision — what happens after signup |
| **Subject line** | "Here's what happens after you sign up" |
| **Content** | Walk through Day 1-2 (setup), Day 3-7 (first win), Day 7-14 (first coaching/support), Day 14-30 (momentum building). End with concrete results by end of month one. Mention pricing again. |
| **CTA** | "Reply '[KEYWORD]' and I'll get you set up within 24 hours. Or reply with any questions." |

### Email 10: The Respectful Close

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Day** | Day 25 |
| **Focus** | Final CTA — respectful last call |
| **Subject line** | "Not going to keep emailing you about this" or similar respect signal |
| **Content** | Acknowledge this is the last email on the topic. Recap the offer briefly (price, what's included, key benefit). Mention other services/products if the timing isn't right. End with a genuine, no-pressure invitation. |
| **CTA** | "Reply '[KEYWORD]' and I'll personally get you set up. Or reply with whatever's holding you back." |

## 4. Tone and Voice Rules

1. **Personal and peer-to-peer.** The reader already knows the sender. Write like a colleague, not a marketer.
2. **Use "[First Name]" placeholder** at the start of each email.
3. **Short paragraphs.** 1-3 sentences. Lots of white space.
4. **No links, no landing pages.** Every CTA is "reply to this email" or "send me a message."
5. **No hype.** No "revolutionary," "game-changing," etc. Let specifics do the work.
6. **Honest and transparent.** Acknowledge limitations. Admit what it can't do.
7. **Use the client's voice.** Their terminology, their industry language, their way of describing things.
8. **Subject lines:** Short (under 8 words), curiosity-driven, no spam triggers (no ALL CAPS, no "FREE," no exclamation marks).
9. **Sign off with just the client's first name.** No titles, no company name in the signature.

## 5. Sending Cadence

| Email | Day | Gap | Focus |
|-------|-----|-----|-------|
| 1 | Day 1 | — | Desire — opportunity awareness |
| 2 | Day 3 | 2 days | Pain — validation of the struggle |
| 3 | Day 5 | 2 days | Pointing — the general approach |
| 4 | Day 8 | 3 days | Product intro — origin story |
| 5 | Day 10 | 2 days | Product details — full breakdown |
| 6 | Day 13 | 3 days | Value anchor — price comparison |
| 7 | Day 16 | 3 days | Pain + urgency — cost of inaction |
| 8 | Day 19 | 3 days | Objection handling — specificity |
| 9 | Day 22 | 3 days | Future pacing — onboarding vision |
| 10 | Day 25 | 3 days | Final CTA — respectful close |

**Notes:**
- Emails 1-3 are spaced closer (2 days) to build momentum before the product reveal
- Emails 4-10 are spaced further (2-3 days) to give the reader time to process
- Total sequence: 25 days

## 6. Content Mapping Checklist

Ensure these elements from the offer appear across the 10 emails:

### Emails 1-3 (Belief Building)
- [ ] Biggest market stat or opportunity number
- [ ] Full audience pain description with specific hours/costs
- [ ] All frustrations and their cascading consequences
- [ ] All failed alternatives mentioned
- [ ] Early adopter results with specific numbers

### Emails 4-6 (Product Introduction)
- [ ] Origin story — why the product was built
- [ ] Product name and clear description
- [ ] All differentiators from generic alternatives
- [ ] ALL package inclusions with descriptions
- [ ] Complete pricing: launch rate, regular rate, payment plan, lifetime option
- [ ] All price anchor comparisons with specific dollar amounts

### Emails 7-10 (Objection Handling + Close)
- [ ] Cost of inaction with specific dollar estimates
- [ ] #1 objection addressed honestly
- [ ] Specific product capabilities that address the objection
- [ ] Honest limitations + how support fills the gap
- [ ] 30-day onboarding walkthrough
- [ ] Final offer recap with all key details
- [ ] Scarcity element (founding member spots, price increase)

### Throughout
- [ ] Client's full credibility mentioned (at least in emails 4 and 8)
- [ ] Specific numbers from the offer used (not rounded or generalized)
- [ ] Reply CTAs on every email (no links)
- [ ] Client's first name sign-off on every email

## 9. Step-by-Step Build Process

1. **Read the offer file** completely. Identify: client name, product name, audience, all sections.
2. **Check for existing warm lead messaging** in the offer. If 7 emails exist, use them as the base and expand to 10.
3. **Identify the strongest opening stat** for Email 1.
4. **Write all 10 emails** in order, following the structure in Section 3.
5. **Verify completeness** against the checklist in Section 6.
6. **Review tone** — every email should sound like a personal note from the client, not a marketing campaign.
7. **Build the HTML version** using the design system and reference implementation.
8. **Output both files** — `.md` and `.html`.

## 10. Adaptation Notes

### Different audience warmth levels
- **Very warm** (past clients, close contacts): More casual, reference shared history, shorter emails
- **Warm** (email list, social followers): As described above — personal but slightly more context-setting
- **Cool-warm** (met at events, loose connections): Add more credibility earlier, slightly longer belief-building phase

### Different product types
- **SaaS/tool**: Focus on time savings and output examples
- **Course/program**: Focus on transformation and curriculum walkthrough
- **Coaching/service**: Focus on access and personalized support
- **Physical product**: Focus on quality and convenience

### Short-notice launches
If the launch window is shorter than 25 days, compress to 7 emails:
- Combine emails 1+2, skip email 3, keep 4-6, combine 7+8, keep 9, keep 10
- Reduce gaps to 1-2 days each

### Multiple audiences
If the offer has two audiences, create separate email sequences for each. The emails will share some structure but differ in language, examples, and pain points.
