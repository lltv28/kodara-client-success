1 Desire · Day 1
The Contract You Turned Down Long-form

You turned down a contract last month.

Not because you couldn't do the work. Not because the money wasn't right. Not because it wasn't a perfect fit for your business.

You turned it down because YOU are the bottleneck.

There literally aren't enough hours in your day to take on one more thing. And your team? They can handle the tasks you assign them. But the thinking, the decision-making, the "how do we actually do this" part — that still lives in your head.

So you said no.

And you told yourself it was the smart move. "We're at capacity." "I don't want to overextend." "Maybe next quarter."

But here's what that really cost you.

That contract wasn't just revenue. It was proof your business could operate at the next level. It was the reference, the case study, the momentum that compounds over years.

I've spent 10 years working with owner-dependent businesses. The ones with 5, 7, 10 employees where the founder is still the hub of every decision.

And the pattern is always the same. The business doesn't fail from lack of demand. It flatlines because the owner can't let go — and there's no structure in place for anyone else to step in.

The ceiling isn't your market. It isn't your product. It isn't your team.

It's you.

And the worst part? You already know this.

If you've turned down work this year because you couldn't personally handle one more thing, drop a comment below. I want to hear what it cost you.

3 Pain · Day 5
Your Last Hire Didn't Fail Because of Them Long-form

Let's talk about the hire that didn't work out.

You found someone solid. Good resume. Said all the right things. You were genuinely excited because you thought, "Finally. Someone who can take things off my plate."

Three months later they were gone. Or worse — still there, but you were doing their thinking for them.

And you told yourself the story every business owner tells: "Nobody can do it like I do."

But that's not what happened.

They didn't fail because they weren't capable. They failed because there was nothing to work with.

No documented process for how you make decisions. No framework for what "good" looks like at your company. No system that captures the way YOU think about the work.

You hired a person and dropped them into chaos. Then blamed the person.

A failed hire at your level costs somewhere between $50,000 and $100,000 when you factor in recruiting, training time, lost productivity, the work that didn't get done, and the damage to your team's morale.

And most owners I talk to? They've done this two or three times.

That's $150K to $300K spent learning the same lesson: people can't run what isn't built.

Your standards aren't the problem. They're the reason your business is good. But standards without structure is just you doing everything forever.

The next hire will fail for the same reason. Unless something changes before they start.

If your last hire didn't work out and you're still not sure why, send me a message. I'll tell you exactly what was missing.

9 Pain + urgency · Day 17
What Staying the Bottleneck Actually Costs Long-form

Let me run some numbers for you.

Every time an employee stops what they're doing to ask you a question they could have answered themselves — that's 15 to 30 minutes of lost productivity. Yours and theirs.

If that happens five times a day across your team, that's roughly 10 to 15 hours a week of interrupted work. Over a year, that's 500 to 750 hours.

At your billing rate, that's easily $50,000 to $150,000 in lost capacity. Every year. Just from your team not knowing how to make decisions without you.

Now add the contract you turned down because you were tapped out. The hire that failed because they had nothing to follow. The vacation you cut short because something went sideways and only you could fix it.

Add the growth you didn't pursue. The partnership you didn't explore. The idea you shelved because "I just don't have the bandwidth right now."

That's not a capacity problem. That's a structural problem.

And it gets worse every year, not better. Because the more your business grows, the more decisions funnel through you. The more people you add, the more they depend on you. The bigger you get, the more trapped you become.

You don't want employees who follow instructions. You want employees who think. But they can't think like you if your way of thinking has never been captured and shared.

This is the problem Bulletproof COO solves. Setup is $1,997 for the first 25 founding members, then $200 a month to keep the system current as your business evolves.

Compare that to the cost of one more year as the bottleneck.
