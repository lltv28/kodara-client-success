**Book:** Fall in Love with Your Voice: A Singer's Journey from Stuck to Sovereign
**Author:** Alisa Larsen

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter is the bridge between philosophy and physiology. Parts 1 and 2 gave the reader the foundation and the framework — the nervous system safety, the 6 C's, the permission to sing from the inside out. Now it is time to go into the body and build the technical ground floor: breath support.

But this is not a conventional lesson in support. It begins by dismantling the single most damaging phrase in voice training: "support more." This instruction, issued by well-meaning teachers in studios everywhere, has created a generation of singers who push harder, tighten their abdomens, muscle through their phrases, and call it support. It is not. It is effort masquerading as technique.

By the end of this chapter, the reader understands what true diaphragmatic support actually feels like — 75 to 85 percent engagement, a softened solar plexus, and a relationship with gravity that allows the breath to do what it was already designed to do. They learn that support is not something you add. It is something you stop interfering with.

This chapter also sets the technical template for everything that follows in Part 3 — mixed belt, throat chakra work, resonance and acoustics. All of it depends on a correct understanding of support. Get this right, and the rest of the technique begins to organize itself naturally. Get this wrong, and every chapter after this will feel like pushing a boulder uphill.

## Opening Hook

She has heard it a hundred times.

"Support more."

"Come on — support it."

"You need more support on those high notes."

And every time she hears it, she does the only thing that makes sense: she pushes. She tightens her stomach. She grips. She tries harder. And for a moment — maybe — the note lands. But she is exhausted. Her throat is tight. Her sound is effortful rather than free. And she has no idea what she just did or how to do it again.

This is the tragedy of "support more." It is one of the most commonly issued and least explained instructions in the history of voice training. Teachers say it because they know something is missing. Students hear it and reach for more tension — because more tension is the only physical language they have been given. The result is two people in the same conversation, talking about two completely different things, and the singer leaving the lesson more confused and more contracted than when she arrived.

True support is not force. It is not compression. It is not a held-in, gripped-down, steel-cored abdomen that nothing can get through. True support is a living, breathing coordination — a gentle, sustained engagement between the diaphragm and the breath that is 75 to 85 percent of what's available, never 100. The remaining 15 to 25 percent is space. Softness. The room for the sound to breathe.

The truth about support is that most singers already have access to it. They just have too much interference in the way.

### Section 1: The Problem with "Support More" — A Diagnosis of the Most Common Vocal Sabotage

**Concept:**
"Support more" fails as an instruction for two reasons. First, it provides no somatic reference — it tells the singer what to do but not what to feel, so the brain reaches for the only available interpretation: more effort, more tension, more control. Second, it conflates support with pressure. In classical training especially, the image of a "supported tone" has become synonymous with a held, projected, pushed sound — a tone that wears its effort visibly. But effort is not support. Effort is the absence of coordination.

Alisa reframes support from a mechanical instruction to a somatic experience. True diaphragmatic support is not something you apply to a note from the outside. It is the physical environment inside which a free note can occur. The diaphragm is not a pump you operate. It is a dome-shaped muscle that descends on the inhale, ascends on the exhale, and can be asked — gently, sustainably — to slow that ascent while sound is moving through. That slowing is support. It is a coordination, not a contraction.

Key distinction: holding in vs. holding on. Most singers when told to "support" hold in — compressing the core, locking the abdomen, cutting off the breath from below. Alisa teaches holding on — a gentle, sustained engagement that maintains the breath column without gripping it. The difference in the body is subtle. The difference in the sound is enormous.

**Example / Story:**
A student arrives having been trained in a classical program for years. She has been told to "support" since her sophomore year. She holds her stomach like a board. She has convinced herself this is correct — it was praised by her teachers — but she cannot sustain a phrase without tension creeping into her throat, cannot soften her sound without feeling like she is losing control, cannot access her lower range without the sound going hollow beneath her. The problem is not her voice. It is that her "support" is a closed fist around the breath. In Alisa's studio, the first thing they do is undo all of it — soften the solar plexus, let the belly release, breathe like a human being again. The student cries. "I've been bracing for years," she says. "I didn't know I was allowed to let go."

**Connection to next section:**
If support is not compression, it must be something else entirely. The next section introduces what it actually is — and what part of the body is doing the work.

### Section 2: The Diaphragm Is Not What You Think — Anatomy Made Visceral

**Concept:**
Most singers have a vague idea that "the diaphragm" is involved in singing. They have been told to "breathe from the diaphragm" and "sing from the diaphragm" without ever being told what the diaphragm actually is, where it lives, or what it does when functioning correctly. This anatomical fog is at the root of the "support more" problem — you cannot coordinate a muscle you have never actually felt.

Alisa demystifies the diaphragm without turning this into a biology lecture. The diaphragm is a dome-shaped sheet of muscle that forms the floor of the rib cage and the ceiling of the abdominal cavity. On the inhale, it contracts downward, creating a vacuum that draws air into the lungs. On the exhale — and during singing — it releases upward. True support asks the diaphragm to delay and regulate that upward release, maintaining the breath column beneath the sound without shutting it down.

The solar plexus — the network of nerves at the center of the abdomen, just below the sternum — is the key to feeling this coordination. When the solar plexus is soft, the diaphragm can move freely. When the solar plexus is gripped (as in anxiety, performance tension, or over-efforting), the diaphragm is restricted, the breath goes shallow, and the singer unconsciously shifts the work into the throat and neck. This is why nervous system regulation (Part 1) is not separate from technique (Part 3). They are the same conversation.

75 to 85 percent engagement means: the diaphragm is doing steady, sustained work, but it is never clenched to maximum. The remaining 15 to 25 percent is the breath's own intelligence — its natural buoyancy, the acoustic resonance of the phrase, the slight give in the body that allows vibration to move freely. Support is a hand on the sail, not a fist around the rope.

**Framework introduced here:**
The Support Triangle (see Frameworks section below).

**Example / Story:**
The Surfer on a Wave — Alisa's recurring image applies perfectly here. The breath is the wave. The diaphragm is the surfer. A surfer does not control the wave; she reads it, positions herself, and allows the wave's energy to carry her. The moment she grabs the wave, tries to direct it, holds it in place — she wipes out. The voice works exactly the same way. The diaphragm's job is to ride the breath, not to own it.

**Connection to next section:**
Understanding the diaphragm anatomically is the beginning. The next step is learning what correct support actually feels like from the inside — and how to find it without manufacturing it.

### Section 3: Letting Gravity Do the Work — The Radical Simplicity of True Support

**Concept:**
One of Alisa's most important and counterintuitive teachings is this: the body already knows how to support. You did not learn to support your breath when you spoke your first words. Your diaphragm coordinated itself. The exhale was regulated without your conscious intervention. The breath moved, the sound moved, and no one told your six-year-old self to "support more."

What happened between childhood and now is that technique — well-intentioned but often misdirected — taught singers to override their body's native intelligence with conscious muscular management. They learned to do what the body already knew how to do, but now with effort, with gripping, with the constant fear of "losing" support. The result is singers who work far harder than necessary to produce sounds that are far smaller than what they are capable of.

Gravity is the singer's secret weapon. When the body is aligned (Belinda the Brontosaurus from Chapter 3), when the nervous system is regulated, when the solar plexus is soft — gravity creates a natural downward pull on the breath that acts as a ground floor for the sound. The singer does not have to push down; she simply has to stop pushing up. She stops interfering with what gravity is already offering, and the support appears.

The 75 to 85 percent target is not a number to aim for consciously. It is the natural level of engagement that emerges when the singer stops over-efforting and lets the breath's own logic take over. The instruction is not "engage more." It is "soften the solar plexus, feel the ground beneath you, and let the diaphragm find its own coordination."

**Practical exercise introduced here:**
The Sigh of Relief — Alisa guides the reader through a practice of releasing all tension in the solar plexus on an exhale, sighing from deep in the body, feeling the belly rise slightly on the inhale without being pushed, and noticing where the breath naturally settles. This is the starting point for every support exercise that follows. Before any vocalization, any scale, any phrase — the Sigh of Relief establishes the home base.

**Example / Story:**
A student who has been told her entire career that her voice is "breathy" — a diagnosis that usually comes with the prescription "support more." In their first session, Alisa has her stop all deliberate engagement and simply sigh. Then hum. Then speak a phrase in her natural speaking voice. The breathiness disappears. Not because she supported more, but because she stopped over-pressurizing the breath from below in an attempt to "fix" it. The breathiness was not a lack of support. It was the result of too much pressure creating turbulence at the vocal folds. Less interference, not more effort. That was all.

**Connection to next section:**
Gravity and alignment create the ground floor. But there is a specific physical landmark — the solar plexus — that either opens or closes the whole system. The next section goes deeper into this hinge point and how to keep it soft.

### Section 4: The Solar Plexus — The Body's Hidden Off Switch

**Concept:**
The solar plexus is not a vocal structure. It is not a muscle. It is a dense network of nerve tissue located at the pit of the stomach, just below the sternum, at the crossroads of the sympathetic nervous system. And yet it is one of the most important physical landmarks in all of Alisa's teaching — because it is the place where fear shows up in the body, and where the singer's entire relationship with support either opens or collapses.

When the singer is anxious, the solar plexus grips. When she is performing for approval, it braces. When she is trying too hard, it clenches. This is not metaphor — it is anatomy. The solar plexus is part of the fight-or-flight response. Under threat (real or imagined — an audience counts), it contracts to protect the core organs. And when it contracts, the diaphragm can no longer move freely. The breath goes shallow. The throat takes on the load. And the singer pushes, because pushing is the only strategy left when the ground floor is locked down.

Softening the solar plexus is therefore both a nervous system practice and a technique practice. It is the point at which the work of Part 1 and Part 2 becomes physically embodied in the singing body. Every time a singer softens her solar plexus before a phrase, she is doing three things simultaneously: regulating her nervous system, allowing the diaphragm to engage naturally, and creating the physical conditions for a free, supported tone.

The instruction is deceptively simple: breathe into the solar plexus. Imagine it as a flower opening. Let it be soft enough that you could press your finger into it without resistance. Not collapsed — soft. Not braced — open. This is the off switch for the body's interference with natural support.

**Connection to the book's larger themes:**
The solar plexus as off switch is a direct physical expression of the book's central argument: that the singer's job is not to add more, but to remove interference. The voice is not improved by effort. It is liberated by release.

**Example / Story:**
Alisa describes a student who came to a group session visibly nervous. Her voice was tight, her tone was thin, her high notes were strained. Alisa asked her to stop singing and put one hand on her solar plexus. "What do you feel?" The student said: "A wall." They spent the next fifteen minutes on nothing but softening that wall — breath, imagery, gentle touch. When she sang again, the room went quiet. Not because she had gotten louder or technically better. Because she had gotten real. Her sound had dimension. The wall had come down.

**Connection to next section:**
The solar plexus opens the system. But true support also requires understanding the relationship between the breath and the sound — specifically, how much air is actually needed, and why more is almost never the answer.

### Section 5: The High Note Lie — Less Air, More Trust

**Concept:**
One of the most persistent and damaging myths in voice training is that high notes require more air. The instruction "support more for high notes" is almost always interpreted as "push more air through for high notes." This creates a pressure-cooker dynamic at the vocal folds — excessive subglottal pressure meeting the narrowing closure of the cords at higher pitches — that produces strain, thinning, or the dreaded register break. Singers who push more air for high notes are working against the physics of their own instrument.

The truth is the opposite: high notes require less air, more coordination, and more trust. As the pitch rises, the vocal folds thin and stretch. They need less breath passing through them, not more. What they need is a steady, calibrated breath column — the 75 to 85 percent engagement — that maintains consistent subglottal pressure without flooding the cords. The singer's job going into a high note is not to gear up; it is to stay steady. To resist the urge to push. To trust the coordination that has been built through consistent practice.

This is one of Alisa's contrarian beliefs from her core teaching, and it lands with force here because the reader has been prepared for it. They understand the nervous system. They understand that effort creates interference. They have met the Honey Pot (the image of dropping support downward at the solar plexus rather than reaching upward for a high note). Now they can understand why: the Honey Pot works because it anchors the support below the pitch instead of pushing it upward into it. The sound floats up because the foundation holds steady below.

**Key reframe:**
High notes are not a problem to be muscled through. They are a question the body asks: "Do you trust me?" The singer who trusts the coordination, who holds the steady 75 to 85 percent and lets the pitch find itself, will access the note. The singer who braces, pushes, and over-engages will fight it every time.

**Example / Story:**
The Honey Pot in action — a student straining for a high note in a song she loves. She is clearly pushing: the effort is visible in her neck, her face, her shoulders. Alisa stops her. "Don't go up. Drop down. Find the honey at your solar plexus." The student closes her eyes, finds the image, softens the solar plexus, imagines the support anchoring down. She sings the note again. It floats. She opens her eyes and laughs. "I wasn't going for it," she says. "I was just — holding on." Exactly. That's support.

**Connection to next section:**
Once the singer understands what support is and what it feels like, the question becomes: how do you build it into the muscle memory, the daily practice, the automatic coordination that doesn't require conscious management in the middle of a performance?

### Section 6: Building Support into the Body — Practice That Becomes Instinct

**Concept:**
True support is not something you think about during a performance. By the time the singer is in front of an audience, the coordination between breath, diaphragm, and sound needs to be so well-established that it runs on its own — a trained automatic, like the instinct of a dancer's core or a swimmer's stroke. The conscious work of this chapter is what makes that automatic possible.

This section bridges the conceptual into the practical, giving the reader the beginning of a daily breath practice that builds the support coordination into muscle memory. The key is that none of these exercises should feel effortful. If they feel effortful, the singer is over-engaging — back to 100 percent, back to the closed fist. The exercises are designed to find the 75 to 85 percent naturally and then stabilize it through repetition.

Alisa also introduces what she calls the "support anchor check" — a three-breath reset that the singer can use before any vocalization, any performance, any moment when support needs to be re-established without conscious effort. It is fast, it is physical, and it works from the outside in and the inside out simultaneously.

Consistency is the key word here (linking back to Chapter 8): not three hours of intense breath work once a week, but five to ten minutes of deliberate support practice every day. That daily repetition is what crosses the threshold from "I know how to support" to "my body supports without being told."

**Practical exercises introduced here:**
1. The Sigh of Relief (re-introduced here in a vocalized form — the sigh becomes an extended exhale on an open vowel, building the experience of supported tone without efforting it)
2. The Hiss Exercise — a sustained hiss on exhale, feeling the diaphragm's steady engagement without pushing, finding the 75 to 85 percent by listening for a steady, unfluctuating hiss rather than one that rushes and fades
3. The Support Anchor Check — three-breath pre-practice reset (see Frameworks section below)

**Example / Story:**
A student six months into Alisa's program who could not access her lower range without the sound going hollow, and could not access her upper range without pushing. After consistent daily practice with the Hiss Exercise and the Sigh of Relief, she reports: "I was singing in the car last week and I realized I wasn't thinking about support at all. It was just there." That is the destination. Not conscious competence, but unconscious competence. The body has learned the coordination and taken ownership of it.

**Connection to next section:**
Support is the foundation. Now the entire instrument — freed from the interference of over-efforting, gripped abdomens, and pushed high notes — is ready to be explored in its full range. The next chapter takes the singer from the bottom of her range to the top without losing the speech quality of her voice. Mixed belt builds on everything established here.

## Frameworks and Models

### The Support Triangle

A simple visual model showing the three elements that make up true support:

- **Diaphragmatic engagement (75-85%)** — the steady, regulated rise of the diaphragm on exhale; not gripped, not collapsed; the engine
- **Soft solar plexus** — the physical and neurological off switch for interference; the open door
- **Aligned body (Belinda the Brontosaurus)** — the container that makes efficient breath coordination possible; the architecture

All three must be present for true support to occur. Remove the soft solar plexus (grip), and the diaphragm cannot move freely. Remove the aligned body, and the breath mechanics are working against the structure. Remove the diaphragmatic engagement, and the breath has no regulation. The triangle only holds when all three sides are intact.

---

### The Interference Scale

A spectrum model that helps the singer understand where they are in their relationship with support:

| Over-Efforting (100%+) | True Support (75-85%) | Under-Supporting (<50%) |
|---|---|---|
| Pushed, compressed, tight | Engaged, buoyant, steady | Breathy, hollow, unsupported |
| Throat takes on extra load | Throat stays free | Breath floods through cords |
| Sound is effortful and strained | Sound is free and resonant | Sound lacks center and focus |
| High notes require pushing | High notes float with less air | High notes thin out or disappear |
| Solar plexus gripped | Solar plexus soft | Solar plexus collapsed |
| Body holds its breath | Body rides the breath | Body lets go of the breath |

Most singers toggle between over-efforting and under-supporting without ever landing in the middle. True support is the middle — and it is available in every practice session once the interference is removed.

---

### The Support Anchor Check (Pre-Practice Reset)

A three-breath sequence to establish support before any vocalization:

1. **Exhale completely** — let everything go; feel the solar plexus soften and the belly release
2. **Inhale through the nose** — allow the belly to rise naturally, without lifting the chest; feel the diaphragm descend into the space below
3. **Exhale on a sustained hiss** — let the hiss be steady and even, not rushed; feel the diaphragm's slow, regulated ascent; notice the 75 to 85 percent engagement without naming it — just feel the difference between this and gripping

Three rounds. Then vocalize.

This is not warm-up. It is orientation. It tells the body where home is before the singing begins.

---

### The High Note Protocol

A reminder card for singers who tend to push into their upper range:

1. **Don't go up — drop down.** Find the honey pot at the solar plexus. Anchor support below the note, not into it.
2. **Use less air.** Resist the urge to add pressure. The cords know what to do with less.
3. **Trust the coordination.** The note is not a problem to be solved. It is a question the body answers when you stop over-answering it.
4. **Stay at 75 to 85 percent.** The engagement does not increase for high notes. The trust does.

## Common Mistakes

**1. Interpreting "support" as "push."**
The most universal error. When told to support more, singers push more air, compress the abdomen, or brace the core. This creates excess subglottal pressure that causes strain, thinning, and throat tension. Support is not push. It is regulation.

**2. Gripping at 100 percent when 75 to 85 percent is required.**
The impulse to fully engage — to make absolutely sure the support is "there" — is the enemy of free tone. Maximum engagement restricts the diaphragm's ability to respond to the acoustic needs of the phrase. The body overcorrects and overtightens. The remaining 15 to 25 percent of space is not wasted — it is necessary.

**3. Over-engaging the abdominals instead of the diaphragm.**
Many singers, when told to "engage the core," suck in the abdomen or brace the six-pack muscles. This is not diaphragmatic support. The diaphragm is internal; the abdominals are external. Over-engaging the abdominals actually restricts the diaphragm's downward movement on inhale and upward regulation on exhale. The belly should be free to move — softly outward on the inhale, softly inward on the sustained exhale — not held.

**4. Adding more air for high notes.**
The opposite of what is needed. High notes require coordinated, calibrated support — not increased pressure. More air creates turbulence at the cords, not lift. The Honey Pot image exists precisely to counteract this instinct.

**5. Trying to feel support rather than letting it happen.**
Over-monitoring — constantly checking whether the support is "there" — is its own form of interference. The Support Anchor Check is designed to establish the coordination before singing begins, not during it. During the phrase, the job is to ride the breath, not audit it.

**6. Separating technique from nervous system state.**
Trying to build correct support while in a state of performance anxiety or nervous system dysregulation is like trying to build a house on sand. The solar plexus will be gripped. The breath will be shallow. The diaphragm will be restricted. The nervous system must be regulated first. This is not sequencing for convenience — it is anatomical reality.

**7. Practicing support as an isolated exercise and never integrating it into singing.**
Doing the Hiss Exercise perfectly in isolation and then reverting to pushing the moment a song begins is the pattern Alisa sees constantly. Integration requires practicing the coordination inside actual phrases, actual songs, actual musical contexts — not just in technical drills.

## Chapter Takeaway

You have been told your whole singing life to "support more." And you have been doing your best to obey an instruction that was never properly explained. You have gripped, compressed, efforting your way through phrases, and called it technique. And you have paid for it — in tension, in strain, in a voice that never quite felt free.

Here is the truth: your body already knows how to support. It has been doing it since the first sound you ever made. What has been in the way is not a lack of support. It is interference — the gripped solar plexus, the braced abdomen, the pushed breath, the over-efforting that technique layered on top of what was already working.

The work of this chapter is not to add something new. It is to remove what has been in the way. To soften the solar plexus. To let gravity do what it was designed to do. To find the 75 to 85 percent — not by gripping harder, but by releasing the grip and letting the body's own intelligence take over.

True support feels like riding a wave, not controlling one. It feels like dropping into honey, not reaching for a note. It feels like the breath knowing what to do because it was always going to, if you just got out of the way.

Stop pushing. Soften. Let the breath carry you. That is support.

**Core sentence of the chapter:**
Support is not what you add to the breath — it is what remains when you stop interfering with it.

## Bridge to Chapter 12: Mixed Belt and the Singer's Sandbox

With a true understanding of support now in the body — the soft solar plexus, the 75 to 85 percent engagement, the Honey Pot anchoring support below the pitch rather than pushing up into it — the reader is ready for the next fundamental challenge of vocal technique: range.

Most singers experience their voice not as one continuous instrument, but as two or three separate instruments living uneasily in the same body: a chest voice that feels solid and real, a head voice that feels thin and uncertain, and a break in between that feels like falling off a cliff. The mission of Chapter 12 is to close that gap — not by forcing the registers to blend, not by drilling transitions until they smooth over, but by preserving the quality of the speaking voice all the way from the bottom of the range to the top.

This is what mixed belt is: not a genre, not a style, but a technical reality. The speech quality of the voice — its naturalness, its presence, its human texture — can be maintained through the entire range when the breath support is correct, the acoustic environment is right, and the body is not forcing a register change that the voice, left to its own coordination, would never make.

The tools for this work — primal sounds, the "Why Not?" exercise, the moan, the sigh, the whine — all build on the support foundation established in Chapter 11. Without true support underneath, mixed belt is impossible. With it, the singer's sandbox opens — and for the first time, the whole voice is available.

Chapter 12 is where the freedom begins.
